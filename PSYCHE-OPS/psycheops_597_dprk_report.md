## THE FINDING

The Hwasong-11A is not a copy.

That single sentence rewrites the risk profile for every air defense system
the West has sold or promised to Ukraine — and it rewrites it in real time,
with active warfare as the test environment.

Ukrainian defense officials confirmed in April 2026 that the KN-23 and KN-24
missiles recovered from Russian strike sites are indigenous North Korean
designs. Not reverse-engineered Iskanders. Not licensed Russian production.
North Korean. Built with commercial electronics and manufacturing methods
decades behind modern standards — soldering quality resembling work from fifty
years ago — and yet flying. Hitting targets. Improving in accuracy since 2023.

The story is not the missile. The story is what the missile proves about
North Korean design capability, and who is now paying attention to that proof.

## CHAIN OF CUSTODY — DEBRIS FLOW

### Strike Site → Ukrainian State Apparatus

Ukrainian state prosecutors examined debris from 21 of approximately 50 North
Korean ballistic missiles launched by Russia between late December and late
February. Roughly half of the missiles lost their programmed trajectories and
exploded in the air — debris in those cases was not recoverable.

Within days of the January 2, 2024 attack on Kharkiv, the regional
prosecutor's office showcased fragments to the media. The assessment: this
was not a Russian model.

Three UN sanctions monitors traveled to Ukraine in April 2024 to inspect the
wreckage firsthand. They found no evidence the missile was Russian-
manufactured. Conclusion: the debris derived from a DPRK Hwasong-11 series
missile, in violation of the 2006 arms embargo on North Korea.

### Ukrainian Technical Intelligence

Internal analysis of KN-23 and KN-24 debris found production techniques
appearing decades behind modern standards — particularly in soldering and
assembly quality, with some workmanship resembling methods from as far back
as fifty years ago.

April 2026 brought the definitive finding: the Hwasong-11A is not a direct
copy or licensed production of Russia's Iskander. The missiles were built
using commercial electronics, outdated manufacturing methods, and less
energy-efficient fuel — requiring larger engines to achieve equivalent range
to Russian counterparts.

**Indigenous design. Confirmed.**

### The Sanctions Violation Layer

Components sourced from foreign manufacturers: Britain, China, Japan,
Switzerland, the United States. One recovered missile contained a voltage
converter bearing the label of British company XP Power, produced in the
same year of deployment. The dual-use export control problem is embedded
in the debris itself.

## INTELLIGENCE SHARING ARCHITECTURE

### United States — Primary Analytical Authority

The Defense Intelligence Agency released an unclassified report in May 2024
confirming the Kharkiv debris as almost certainly a DPRK short-range missile.
Method: open-source imagery analysis — missile factory images from North
Korean state media cross-referenced against strike wreckage. The cable tray
and handling ring connectors matched exactly.

Unclassified by design. The finding was meant to be seen.

### MSMT — The Multilateral Node

Russia vetoed the renewal of the UN Security Council's 1718 Committee Panel
of Experts in March 2024, disbanding the fifteen-year sanctions monitoring
mechanism. The Multilateral Sanctions Monitoring Team was established in
October 2024 to fill that gap.

Eleven participating states:

```
Australia · Canada · France · Germany · Italy · Japan
Netherlands · New Zealand · Republic of Korea · United Kingdom · United States
```

The first MSMT report, released May 2025, focused on unlawful DPRK-Russia
military cooperation including arms transfers. Included: published images of
Hwasong-11A debris collected in Ukraine. This is the formal multilateral
record that South Korea and Japan are both party to.

### South Korea

Seoul has active intelligence collection on the Russia-DPRK technology
exchange loop — not just the missile transfers. South Korean intelligence
reported in September 2025 that Russia supplied North Korea with two or three
submarine propulsion systems, potentially including an operational nuclear
reactor and associated components.

As an MSMT member, South Korea has formal access to the debris analysis
underpinning the accuracy-improvement findings Ukrainian sources flagged in
February 2025: North Korean ballistic missile accuracy dramatically improved
since North Korea entered the war.

### Japan

Japan's Defense Ministry confirmed DPRK launches in January 2026, with
Defense Minister Shinjiro Koizumi stating Japan was coordinating closely with
the United States and South Korea. As an MSMT member, Japan is formally
inside the debris intelligence loop.

No public statement from the Japan Ministry of Defense specifically
referencing technical analysis of Ukraine debris has surfaced in open sources.

## SIGNAL ASSESSMENT

The core claim is confirmed by open sources as of April 2026. The Hwasong-11A
is an indigenous North Korean design. The intelligence-sharing chain through
MSMT formally places South Korea and Japan inside the loop.

The debris chain of custody is fully documented:

```
Kharkiv prosecutor's office
    → UN sanctions monitors (on-site inspection, April 2024)
    → DIA (unclassified report, May 2024)
    → MSMT (formal multilateral record, May 2025)
    → Ukrainian technical report (April 2026, indigenous design confirmed)
```

The unresolved layer: what has been done with the finding at the defense
ministry level in Seoul and Tokyo. Whether classified bilateral channels
exist beyond the MSMT's public reporting. Whether the accuracy-improvement
data has triggered updated threat profiles for the Cheongung-II and Patriot
deployments South Korea operates domestically and exports.

The missile is the proof of concept. The threat profile update is the story.
